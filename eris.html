Mother told me again tonight that there is an order to things.

She did not say it gently, though she likes to believe she does. Nyx can make anything sound soft if she lowers her voice enough. Darkness wraps itself around a warning and tries to pass for comfort. But I heard the warning.

"Respect the order," she said.

As if order were a person at table with us.

As if it needed my respect.

As if it had ever once made room for me.

We were at supper, if it can be called that when immortals mostly pick at fruit and drink from cups that never empty. Hemera had already gone. Mother seems calmer after Day leaves, as though she can breathe again when the brightness is gone from the halls. The shadows were long and blue around her shoulders. She looked beautiful, which made it worse.

She only gets beautiful like that when she is about to say something cruel in a way she believes is necessary.

I asked her why some names are spoken openly and others are swallowed.

I asked why Zeus is discussed as though he were the weather itself, why Aunt Nemesis can be praised for balance, why old powers are honored if they are useful, feared if they are not, and never denied at all.

Then I asked why no one says his name.

Not even here.

Not even in our own house.

That was when her hand stilled on the cup.

For a moment I thought she had not heard me, though mothers hear everything that matters and much that does not. Then she looked at me, not angry at first, only tired. Tired in that ancient way gods become when they are forced to repeat a lesson they think should already be understood.

"There is an order to the world," she said. "You are old enough to learn that."

I laughed, which was a mistake.

Old enough. That is what they say when they mean obedient enough.

I asked her if order was the same thing as truth.

She did not like that.

Her mouth became a hard line. The torchlight bent strangely behind her, as if even fire knew better than to stand too straight in her presence.

"Truth is not always yours to dig up," she said. "Some things are buried because they belong buried."

Belong buried. Imagine saying that in a house made of night.

I wanted to ask whether I belonged buried too.

I wanted to ask whether I was one more family embarrassment to be wrapped in velvet silence and set on a high shelf where no one important would have to look directly at me.

But I did not. I am learning that questions have shapes, and some come back as knives.

So I asked instead why I should respect an order that does not respect me.

That was when she stood.

Not all at once. Slowly. More frightening that way.

The room seemed to grow larger and smaller at the same time. Mother does that without trying. She fills space by withdrawing from it. I felt suddenly very young, and I hated her for making me feel it.

"You think yourself wounded by every boundary," she said. "You think being denied a thing makes you the center of the story."

I nearly said, Maybe it does.

I am glad I did not.

Then she stepped nearer and touched my chin, forcing me to meet her eyes. There was no anger there then. Only something worse.

Fear.

I know fear. I know how it smells on mortals and how it sharpens gods. I have seen it in Ares before battle and in lesser spirits when they sense a storm coming. I had never seen it in Mother when she looked at me.

"Listen to me, Eris," she said. "There are powers older than grievance. Older than longing. Older than you can imagine. You will not go looking into places where even the gods avert their gaze. You will not speak to anyone about this. You will not make a game of what came before."

What came before.

Not who.

Not him.

Not grandfather.

Not Chaos.

Just what came before, as though he were a crack in the floor one steps over without comment.

I pulled away from her hand.

That made her face change. Not softer. Colder.

"I am not a child," I said.

"No," she said. "That is exactly the danger."

I wish I could say I answered with dignity. I did not. I said something bitter and bright and stupid. I said order was just another word older gods used when they were afraid of being questioned. I said maybe the whole world was arranged like a feast seating chart, each precious name in its proper place, while anything inconvenient was shut outside the door.

Her expression flickered then, only for a moment, but I saw it.

I had struck something true.

That is the miserable gift of growing older. You begin to hear where silence lives inside other people's sentences.

Mother told me to leave the table.

I did, though not quickly enough to satisfy either of us.

In the corridor, I could still feel the heat of her gaze between my shoulders. The palace was quiet. Too quiet. Night should feel like a cloak, but tonight it felt like a lid.

Respect the order.

I have been turning the words over and over since.

I think what she meant was this: respect the arrangement, the hierarchy, the beautiful lie that everything sits where it ought to sit. Gods above. Titans below. Names spoken. Names forbidden. Mothers guarding doors. Daughters pretending not to notice them.

But if order must be defended with silence, how strong can it be?

And if there truly is nothing worth finding, why is everyone so frightened I might look?

I am ashamed to admit this, but part of me wanted her to strike me, or curse me, or at least shout. Something plain. Something honest. Instead she gave me restraint and warning and fear dressed up as wisdom.

That is how adults keep power, I think.

Not by answering questions.

By teaching you to doubt your right to ask them.

I will be good for a while.

Good enough that she stops watching every doorway when I pass.

Good enough that the house grows careless around me.

Good enough that when I ask harmless things, no one hears the sharp edge underneath.

But I will not forget the look on her face when I said the feast seating chart.

I will not forget that she called him what came before.

I will not forget that fear.

There is an order to things, Mother says.

Perhaps.

But if I ever find the hand that arranged it, I would like to ask why I was placed where I am.

And if no one will answer, then one day I may have to ask more loudly.
